Reclaiming our story: one student at a time.

Diverse stories. Told by us for us.

iFeminist empowers students by exposing them to modern women in history as well as enabling them to conduct their own research. iFeminist encourages research on diverse women from around the world and throughout time to change the narrative regarding women's contributions to society. Each article contributes to a larger database of women in history. We are high school students aimed at changing the face of history by conducting our own research projects.

Our Team

Saba Mehrzad

Founder, Editor in Chief, and Head Software Developer

Saba is a junior at Syosset High School in Syosset, New York.

She first started studying women in history when she was accepted as an intern at the New Historia under Women's Studies Professor Dr. Walker. She is excited to learn more about the women that paved the way for her! Once she started studying Sedigheh Dolatabadi, she started to truly love, cherish, and appreciate her background because of Dolatabadi's amazing accomplishments. Inspired by The New Historia, Saba created iFeminist in order to allow her writers/readers to feel empowered by their identity and feels as though studying Iranian women in history did that for her.

Outside of iFeminist, Saba is the Teasurer of her school's Feminist Club, President of the Breaking Borders Club that aims to desegregate Long Island, NY, and the Founder and President of her school's Womxn in STEM Club. Saba learned how to code this website with the lessons learned at Kode with Klossy! She is also the Founder and President of the Iranian American Society Teen Chapter to celebrate her culture and is a student teacher at her local Farsi school.

Saba's work under Dr. Walker focused on Iranian journalist and philanthropist, Sedigheh Dolatabadi. It is published here! Check it out on the Discoveries page.

Pooja Huded

Contributor

Pooja is a junior at Syosset High School in Syosset, New York.

She has always been fascinated by history and especially likes learning about strong women from her Indian heritage. When she visited India as a child, she would always pass a statue of a woman warrior on a horse while going to visit her cousins. Although her grandma would always explain the history behind the statue, Pooja never felt satisfied with her grandma's answer and wanted to learn more than what her grandma could tell her. Since then, Pooja has always been interested in learning more about India’s rich past and its unsung heroines.

In her free time, Pooja is a part of her school’s Indian Culture Club and runs Cross Country and Track throughout the year. In her research class, Pooja is studying the effect of reserved seats for women in national government on women’s equality in sixty countries, and whether gender quotas are doing enough to increase female representation in the government.

Her work can be found on the Discoveries page!
